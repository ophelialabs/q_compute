In a solenoid, the Poynting vector \(\mathbf{S}=\frac{1}{\mu _{0}}(\mathbf{E}\times \mathbf{B})\) describes the flow of electromagnetic energy into or out of the device when the current changes over time.

[]

Key Characteristics

  • Energy Flow Direction: For an increasing current, the Poynting vector points radially inward toward the center of the solenoid, indicating energy is being stored in the magnetic field. For a decreasing current, it points radially outward, showing energy is being released.
  • Induced Electric Field: Since the current is changing, a circumferential (azimuthal) electric field (\mathbf) is induced according to Faraday’s Law.
  • Magnetic Field: Inside an ideal long solenoid, the magnetic field (\mathbf) is uniform and directed along the axis ((z)-direction), calculated as (B=\mu _{0}nI).
  • Mathematical Expression: The Poynting vector at the inner surface (radius (R)) of the solenoid is typically expressed as:(\mathbf=-\frac{1}{2}\mu _{0}n^{2}RI\frac^{\mathbf}) This vector represents the power per unit area entering the solenoid volume.

Physical Significance

The Poynting Theorem confirms that the total flux of the Poynting vector through the surface of the solenoid equals the rate of change of the energy stored in the magnetic field ((\frac(\frac{1}{2}LI^{2}))). This demonstrates that electrical energy is not carried “inside” the wires but is transferred through the surrounding electromagnetic fields.

The Poynting vector for an infinite solenoid with radius (R), turns per unit length (n), and time-varying current (I(t)) is (\mathbf=-\frac{1}{2}\mu _{0}n^{2}rI\frac^{\mathbf}) for any internal radius (r\le R).  \
