## DNN and how it integrates (Stimulation & Curtain)
1. Deep Neural Network (DNN) Stimulation in Fiber Interfaces 
Flexible graphene-based fiber interfaces allow for high-precision, bidirectional communication with the brain, often integrated with DNNs for signal decoding and closed-loop control. 

- High-Fidelity Interaction: Graphene fibers, with diameters comparable to single neurons, allow for deep brain stimulation (DBS) that targets specific nodes within a neural network rather than broad populations.
- Bidirectional Closed-Loop: Advanced interfaces use on-chip NPUs (Neural Processing Units) to run DNNs that interpret real-time neural biomarkers (e.g., Parkinsonian tremors) and adjust stimulation parameters instantly.
- Material Advantage: The high charge injection capacity (up to 10 mC/cm²) of platinum-coated or porous graphene fibers ensures that electrical pulses can reach deep structures without damaging surrounding tissue.
- Optogenetic Integration: Because graphene is transparent across a broad spectrum, these fiber interfaces can simultaneously perform optical stimulation (via the blue spectrum) and electrical recording, allowing researchers to map functional brain responses in real-time. 

2. Blue Spectrum: Benefits & Cons **Place**
The blue spectrum is the high-energy "engine" of modern optogenetic interfaces. 

Feature 	Benefits	Cons
Performance	Triggers quantum dots efficiently; enables extreme peak brightness (up to 10,000 nits).	High-energy blue light causes faster material degradation in organic and quantum dot layers.
Cognitive	Increases alertness, memory retention, and neural efficiency during the day.	Exposure at night suppresses melatonin, disrupting circadian rhythms and sleep quality.
Interface	Essential for optogenetics; 473nm blue light is the standard for activating Channelrhodopsin-2 (ChR2).	Potential for phototoxicity and local heat generation if not precisely pulsed.
