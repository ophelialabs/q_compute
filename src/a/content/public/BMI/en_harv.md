Authorizing moisture-driven fiber neural interfaces on the Department of Defense Information Network (DoDIN) requires navigating a rigorous compliance framework. Because these devices combine medical functions with network connectivity, they are subject to both information technology (IT) and Internet of Medical Things (IoMT) security standards. 
1. Risk Management Framework (RMF) Authorization 
The primary pathway for any device connecting to the DoDIN is the 7-step RMF process, governed by DoDI 8510.01.
Categorization: Systems are categorized based on the impact of a breach to Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability (CIA).
Control Selection: Neural interfaces must implement specific NIST SP 800-53 security controls tailored for bio-electronic data.
ATO (Authorization to Operate): A designated Authorizing Official (AO) must formally accept the residual risk before the device can transmit data on the network. 

2. DoDIN Approved Products List (APL)
To be eligible for purchase and operation across the DoD, the hardware must typically be listed on the DoDIN APL.
Interoperability Certification: Ensures the fiber interface can communicate with existing DoDIN infrastructure without causing network instability.
Cybersecurity Testing: Conducted at a DISA Testing Center, this involves rigorous vulnerability scanning and penetration testing.
Sunset Transition: Note that the DoDIN APL is currently being sunset (phasing out by September 2025) in favor of more dynamic, continuous monitoring processes. 

3. Medical Device Cybersecurity (MedCOI)
Since these fibers act as neural interfaces, they must adhere to Defense Health Agency (DHA) protocols for the Medical Community of Interest (MedCOI). 

Tailored RMF: Standard IT RMF is often adjusted for Medical Device Equipment (MDE) to ensure security measures do not interfere with patient safety or real-time neural modulation.
Electronic Medical Device (EMD) Review: If used in secure areas (like a SCIF), devices undergo a 30-day review to assess physical and technical security mitigations. 
4. Supply Chain & Contractor Compliance (CMMC 2.0) 
The entities developing and maintaining these neural interfaces must meet Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification (CMMC) standards. 
Level 3 Certification: Given that neural data is often considered "breakthrough, unique, or advanced technology," contractors likely require Level 3 CMMC, involving government-led assessments against NIST SP 800-172.
Phase 1 Rollout: As of November 10, 2025, CMMC requirements are being actively included in all new DoD solicitations. 
5. Technical Implementation Standards
STIG Compliance: Systems must follow Security Technical Implementation Guides (STIGs) for the underlying operating system and application layers.
PPSM (Ports, Protocols, and Services Management): All network traffic generated by the neural interface must be cataloged and governed under DoDI 8551.01 to prevent unauthorized data exfiltration.
